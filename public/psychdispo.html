Tier 1 · The safety gate
1A · Suicide risk assessment

Purpose: Identify patients at elevated suicide risk so you can set level of care and complete required safety interventions before discharge. Assessment stratifies acute, modifiable risk — it does not reliably predict who will die by suicide. The standard of care is an adequate, structured, documented assessment, not accurate prediction.

Screening vs full assessment

  • Screening (C-SSRS Screener, ASQ, PHQ-9 Item 9): any trained clinician using a validated tool; documents a positive or negative screen
  • Full suicide risk assessment: structured evaluation of ideation, intent, plan, means, preparatory behaviors, protective factors, collateral, and acute vs chronic risk — requires formal training in structured suicide risk evaluation
  • If you lack formal training: do not independently assign final risk level or disposition after a positive screen, unclear risk, or moderate/high C-SSRS category. Obtain emergency psychiatry evaluation — psychiatry consult or in-person ED evaluation by a psychiatrist (or equivalently trained behavioral-health clinician per local policy). Maintain safety and document pending psychiatric assessment.

Minimum psychiatry evaluation triggers (any one)

  • C-SSRS moderate or high, or ASQ any "Yes"
  • PHQ-9 Item 9 anything other than "Not at all" (screen positive — needs fuller assessment; not sufficient alone for discharge)
  • Active suicidal ideation with plan, intent, preparatory behavior, or access to lethal means
  • Diagnostic uncertainty, impaired judgment, or inability to complete a collaborative safety plan
  • Disposition other than low-risk outpatient discharge is being considered

Clinical assessment elements (full assessment)

  • Ask directly and nonjudgmentally; use a graded suicide ladder: wish to be dead → thoughts → method → intent → how close they have come
  • Clarify plan, intent, means access, preparatory acts, timing, and expected rescue/lethality
  • Distinguish static baseline risk (prior attempts, trauma, diagnosis) from dynamic acute risk (hopelessness, psychosis, agitation, insomnia, intoxication, acute loss) — treat dynamic factors now
  • Identify protective factors: reasons for living, social support, treatment engagement, means already restricted
  • Obtain collateral when feasible; do not accept disavowal at face value once risk is elevated

C-SSRS Screener — severity 0–5 + behaviors1,5

  • 6 core questions: wished dead · thoughts of killing self · thought how · some intent to act · plan + intent · ever done/started/prepared anything (→ if yes, within 3 months?)
  • LOW: Yes to Q1/Q2 only; Q3–Q6 all No
  • MODERATE: Yes to Q3 (how) OR behavior (Q6) >3 months ago
  • HIGH: Yes to Q4 (intent) and/or Q5 (plan + intent), OR behavior within 3 months
  • Positive screen → +LR >10 for suicide death at 3 mo; in ED, +LR >10 for attempt within 30 days. 2024 VA/DoD: may be the most useful universal-screening tool

PHQ-9 Item 9 — "better off dead / hurting yourself"

  • Any response other than "Not at all" is a positive screen — not a stand-alone risk rating
  • ~5% of primary-care depression patients report SI most/nearly every day → ~1% self-harm/attempt over 90 days8
  • Item 9 alone (vs full PHQ-9) not separately validated for disposition

ASQ — 4-item peds + adult ED screen4

  • Wished dead · family better off if dead · thoughts of killing self · ever tried
  • Any Yes → positive → full assessment; if all No → "thoughts right now?" (acuity). Joint Commission-recommended

Stanley-Brown Safety Planning — 6 steps before discharge11

  • (1) warning signs · (2) internal coping · (3) people/settings for distraction · (4) people to ask for help · (5) professionals/agencies · (6) make environment safe (lethal means)
  • Reduces suicidal behaviors 45–76% vs usual care; meta-analysis of 14 studies → fewer attempts + better follow-up
  • Superior to no-suicide contracts (no evidence; not recommended)

Lethal means counseling (Step 6)

  • Assess firearms, medication stockpiles, sharps, other means
  • Firearms → off-site storage with trusted person, gun lock, safe
  • Medications → limit supply, trusted person holds. Recommended by VA/DoD, AAP, Joint Commission
Disposition logic (by structured C-SSRS level — not PHQ-9 Item 9 alone)
  • LOW → outpatient plan + Stanley-Brown safety plan + lethal-means counseling + crisis resources + follow-up within 7 days; if assessor lacks formal training and any screen was positive, obtain psychiatric evaluation before discharge
  • MODERATE → safety plan + lethal-means restriction required; consider PHP/IOP; follow-up 48–72 h; psychiatric evaluation before discharge unless a formally trained assessor documents safe disposition
  • HIGH → do not discharge without psychiatric evaluation and a contained plan: inpatient (voluntary/involuntary), crisis stabilization, or ED observation; consider 1:1 if imminent risk

Note: Positive screening or elevated concern requires psychiatric assessment before final disposition unless your institution designates you as a clinician with formal suicide-risk-assessment training.

1B · Violence / homicide risk

Purpose: Identify acute risk to others; determine duty-to-warn obligations and disposition. No single tool reliably predicts ED violence — use structured assessment and document reasoning.

Structured tools (forensic/inpatient)13

  • HCR-20 — 20-item historical/clinical/risk-management
  • STAMP — Staring, Tone, Anxiety, Mumbling, Pacing
  • Brøset — confusion, irritability, boisterousness, verbal threats, physical threats, attacks on objects

Key risk factors

  • History of violence (strongest predictor)
  • Active psychosis with command hallucinations / persecutory delusions
  • Intoxication (stimulants, PCP, alcohol); weapon access; specific identified victim; treatment non-adherence

Tarasoff / duty to warn12

  • Obligation (varies by state) to warn identifiable victims of a credible threat
  • Document: specific threat, identified victim, actions taken (victim + law-enforcement notification)

Who should assess

  • Any clinician may screen for acute agitation/threats; formal violence-risk stratification for disposition is best performed by clinicians with emergency psychiatry training or under psychiatric consultation when risk is elevated or unclear
Disposition logic
  • No acute risk → standard outpatient disposition with documentation
  • Elevated, no specific threat → enhanced safety planning, consider higher LOC, psychiatric consultation if untrained assessor, document reasoning
  • Specific threat + identifiable victim → duty to warn/protect per state law, consider involuntary hold, notify law enforcement, psychiatric evaluation before discharge

Note: State Tarasoff/duty-to-warn laws vary — verify local legal requirements.

1C · Agitation management

Purpose: De-escalate behavioral emergencies safely; treat reversible drivers before disposition. Pharmacologic sedation is adjunctive — not a substitute for medical evaluation or psychiatric assessment when indicated.

De-escalation (first-line, always before meds)13

  • Calm voice, short sentences; offer choices (food/drink/seating)
  • Address unmet needs; clear respectful limits; safe distance; remove audience/stimulation; low-stimulation room

Pharmacologic — IM (cannot take PO)15,16

  • Olanzapine 10 mg IM (2.5–5 mg elderly) — preferred SGA for psychosis-driven agitation; do NOT combine with IM lorazepam/Ativan (respiratory depression/hypotension)
  • Haloperidol 5–10 mg IM ± lorazepam 2 mg IM (Haldol + Ativan) — go-to when limited history; may repeat q30–60 min per protocol
  • Haloperidol 5–10 mg IM ± diphenhydramine 50 mg — if avoiding benzos (EPS risk lower with diphenhydramine)
  • Midazolam 5 mg IM — consider for stimulant intoxication or alcohol/benzo withdrawal (not with IM olanzapine)

Oral (cooperative)

  • Olanzapine 5–10 mg ODT; or risperidone 2 mg + lorazepam 2 mg PO

Monitoring & safety

  • Continuous pulse ox (no supplemental O2), VS q15 min until responsive, ETCO2 when available; watch respiratory depression, hypotension, EPS/dystonia, oversedation
  • Hard stop: IM olanzapine + IM lorazepam/Ativan — do not co-administer
  • Antipsychotics → psychosis-driven or undifferentiated agitation; benzos → alcohol/benzo withdrawal & stimulant intoxication (haloperidol + Ativan acceptable; olanzapine + Ativan is not)
Disposition logic
  • Resolved with de-escalation → proceed to psychiatric/medical assessment; do not discharge solely because agitation stopped
  • Requires sedation → observe until clinically responsive; reassess capacity, SI/HI, and medical status before disposition
  • Persistent or recurrent agitation → treat underlying cause (psychosis, intoxication, withdrawal, delirium, pain); psychiatric consultation; admit if unsafe for discharge

Note: Follow institutional restraint/sedation protocols and monitoring standards.

1D · Medical clearance (medical screening exam)

Purpose: Rule out or stabilize medical conditions mimicking or complicating psychiatric presentation before psychiatric disposition. Prefer medical screening exam over "medical clearance."

AAEP minimum for ALL psychiatric patients17,18

  • Thorough history (substances, meds, medical) · physical exam · vital signs incl. temperature · mental status exam

Labs — not routine if alert/cooperative, normal vitals, noncontributory history, normal exam19

  • Fingerstick glucose (AMS/diabetes) · urine pregnancy (childbearing age) · BAL (suspected intox)
  • UDS (rarely changes acute mgmt) · CMP (metabolic/renal) · CBC (infection) · TSH (new-onset)
  • CK — agitation/stimulants/restraint (most common cause of medical intervention in one study) · drug levels (Li/VPA/CBZ)

Screening tools21

  • SMART Checklist — sensitivity 83.3%, NPV 99.1% · TAPS sens 66.6% · Shah's spec 76.9% (all need validation)

Red flags → full workup first

  • Abnormal vitals · altered consciousness/delirium · new-onset psych symptoms age >40 without history · focal deficits · head trauma · significant comorbidities · first-episode psychosis
Disposition logic
  • Active medical condition → stabilize on medical service first; psychiatry consult as needed but do not send delirium/medical instability to psychiatric facility alone
  • Medically stable for psych evaluation → proceed to psychiatric assessment and disposition planning
  • Borderline/uncertain → document reasoning; consider observation, additional workup, or specialty consultation

Note: This tool is for medically cleared patients — complete medical screening before psychiatric disposition.

1E · Capacity evaluation

Purpose: Determine whether the patient can make a specific treatment or disposition decision at this moment. Capacity is decision- and time-specific; it is not the same as competency (a court determination).

Appelbaum 4 components12

  • (1) Understanding · (2) Appreciation (applies to them) · (3) Reasoning (weigh risks/benefits) · (4) Communication of a consistent choice

Principles

  • Can fluctuate — reassess as clinical status changes
  • Disagreeing with advice ≠ lacking capacity; psychiatric illness ≠ automatic incapacity
  • Neurocognitive disorders are the most common cause of impairment
  • A refusal to engage is not a demonstration of capacity

Process

  • Identify the concern (consent vs refusal vs AMA vs dispositional)
  • Psychiatric interview + cognitive assessment; patient describes treatment/risks/benefits/alternatives/consequences in own words; assess each component; consider MacCAT-T
  • Document the specific decision, diagnosis, each component, reversibility, recommendation

Who should assess

  • Any treating clinician may assess capacity for low-complexity decisions; formal capacity evaluation for AMA discharge, refusal of psychiatric care, or high-risk decisions should involve psychiatry or a clinician with documented capacity-assessment training when impairment is suspected
Disposition logic
  • Has capacity → patient decides (including AMA); document shared decision-making and offered alternatives
  • Lacks capacity → surrogate/proxy if available; consider guardianship; involuntary hold if safety concern and less-restrictive options insufficient
  • Uncertain → psychiatry consultation; treat reversible causes (delirium, intoxication) and reassess

Note: State law governs surrogate decision-making and involuntary treatment.

1F · Involuntary commitment

Purpose: Identify when less-restrictive alternatives are insufficient to protect the patient or others. Criteria, duration, and process vary by state — this is an educational framework only.

Criteria (typically require ALL)

  • Mental illness (intoxication alone may not qualify) · imminent danger to self/others OR gravely disabled · less-restrictive alternatives insufficient

Hold types

  • Emergency hold (e.g., 72-h, physician-initiated) · judicial commitment (court-ordered, longer) · outpatient commitment / AOT

Document & rights

  • Specific behaviors/statements showing danger; diagnosis; why less-restrictive insufficient; signature + time
  • Patients retain rights (attorney, hearing, refuse some treatments); state law governs duration, who initiates, "gravely disabled," intoxication holds

Clinical role

  • Initiation often requires an evaluating physician; psychiatric consultation is recommended when criteria are borderline or diagnosis/disposition is uncertain
Disposition logic
  • Criteria not met → voluntary treatment, safety planning, outpatient follow-up, or higher LOC by consent
  • Criteria met → emergency hold per state law; arrange psychiatric admission or crisis stabilization; document specific dangerous behavior
  • Borderline → psychiatry consult; observation; treat modifiable risk; reassess serially

Note: Verify your state's commitment statutes and hospital policy before initiating a hold.

Tier 2 · Level-of-care determination
2A · LOCUS (psychiatric level of care)

Purpose: Match psychiatric intensity to multidimensional need — risk, function, comorbidity, environment, history, and engagement. LOCUS supports disposition planning; it does not replace clinical judgment.

Six dimensions (each 1–5)22

  • Risk of harm · functional status · medical/addictive/psychiatric comorbidity · recovery environment (stress) · treatment & recovery history · engagement & recovery status

Six levels

  • 1 Recovery maintenance (<1 visit/mo) · 2 Low-intensity community (weekly/biweekly) · 3 High-intensity community (IOP/ACT/day tx)
  • 4 Medically monitored non-residential (PHP) · 5 Medically monitored residential (residential/crisis stabilization) · 6 Medically managed residential (inpatient)

Scoring (total 6–30, approx)

  • 6–11→L1 · 12–15→L2 · 16–19→L3 · 20–22→L4 · 23–25→L5 · 26–30→L6
  • Any single dimension = 5 may warrant L5/L6 regardless of total. Joint Commission-recognized
Disposition logic
  • L1–L2 → outpatient with scheduled follow-up
  • L3 → IOP/ACT/high-intensity community
  • L4 → PHP
  • L5 → residential or crisis stabilization
  • L6 → inpatient; combine with Tier 1A suicide-risk level — high acute suicide risk overrides lower total score

Note: Use alongside safety screening (Tier 1) and institutional placement criteria.

2B · ASAM criteria (substance use level of care)

Purpose: Determine appropriate SUD level of care using six biopsychosocial dimensions. Especially important when psychiatric and substance-use needs overlap.

Six dimensions (4th ed.)25

  • Acute intoxication/withdrawal · biomedical conditions · psychiatric & cognitive · substance-use risks · recovery environment · person-centered (SDOH, preferences, motivation)

Levels

  • 1 Outpatient · 1.5 OP therapy · 1.7 Medically managed OP · 2.1 IOP · 2.5 High-intensity OP · 2.7 Medically managed IOP
  • 3.1 / 3.5 / 3.7 / 3.7-BIO residential · 4 Medically managed inpatient (x.7 = withdrawal management incl. benzo)

Principles

  • Person-centered, shared decisions; all programs co-occurring-capable; chronic-care model
  • Should NOT justify administrative discharge. VA/DoD: placement-outcome trials lacking — "neither for nor against," useful as multidimensional assessment
Disposition logic
  • Mild withdrawal/intoxication resolved → outpatient SUD (ASAM 1–2) + psychiatric follow-up if co-occurring
  • Moderate withdrawal or unstable environment → IOP (2.1–2.7) or residential (3.x)
  • Severe withdrawal, medical comorbidity, or high suicide risk → medically managed inpatient (ASAM 4 / 3.7); psychiatric evaluation for co-occurring disorder

Note: Benzo and alcohol withdrawal may require medical admission regardless of psychiatric LOC.

2C · Inpatient admission criteria

Purpose: Identify when less-restrictive settings cannot safely manage acute psychiatric risk or medical instability.

General indications29

  • Active SI with plan/intent (esp. recent attempt); active HI with identified victim + plan
  • Acute psychosis unable to self-care/dangerous; acute mania with dangerous behavior; severe self-neglect / grave disability
  • Med stabilization not safe outpatient (e.g., clozapine init); failed step-down with deterioration
  • Severe alcohol/sedative withdrawal (CIWA-Ar ≥20, DTs/seizure history); eating disorder with medical instability (BMI <15); catatonia

Factors associated with admission from ED30

  • SI/attempt · psychosis · involuntary presentation · prior hospitalization · intoxication + psych symptoms · homelessness
Disposition logic
  • Meets criteria + high suicide risk → inpatient or crisis stabilization; psychiatric evaluation required before discharge alternative
  • Meets criteria but improving in contained ED → consider crisis unit/PHP if safe; document risk trajectory
  • Does not meet criteria → outpatient/IOP/PHP with safety plan per Tier 1A

Note: Bed availability and payer criteria also affect placement — document clinical reasoning.

2D · PHP & IOP

Purpose: Provide structured intermediate intensity between outpatient and inpatient for patients who need more than weekly visits but not 24-hour hospital care.

PHP — ≥20 h/wk (≈5–6 h/day × 5)32

  • Multidisciplinary; intensive group + individual + meds; home each evening
  • Step-down from inpatient (most common), step-up from outpatient, stabilized acute suicidality needing monitoring, impairment too severe for IOP
  • >90% report symptom reduction

IOP — 9–19 h/wk (≈3 h/day × 3–5)34

  • Primarily group; step-down from PHP/inpatient, step-up from outpatient, crisis stabilization, SUD IOP
  • Goal: crisis management/stabilization; cost-effective; equal/superior satisfaction in some studies
Disposition logic
  • Moderate suicide risk, stable, collaborative → IOP/PHP with safety plan + lethal-means counseling + 48–72 h follow-up
  • High suicide risk or poor engagement → not appropriate as first-line; admit or crisis stabilization first
  • Step-down from inpatient → PHP preferred when still needs daily structure; IOP when lower intensity sufficient

Note: Confirm program accepts patient's insurance and acuity before discharge.

2E · Crisis stabilization units

Purpose: Short-term observation and stabilization as an alternative to inpatient admission when crisis may resolve with brief structured intervention.

  • Short-term (<24–72 h), 24-hour-staffed observation as inpatient alternative; voluntary or involuntary
  • Brief assessment, med initiation, safety planning, outpatient linkage; diverts admission, reduces ED boarding
  • Indications: acute crisis that may resolve with brief intervention · SI without imminent plan/intent (observe to clarify) · intoxication needing observation · diagnostic uncertainty (medical vs psych) · no inpatient bed (bridge)
Disposition logic
  • Appropriate → transfer when patient needs observation beyond ED capacity but not full inpatient medical management
  • Not appropriate → high/imminent suicide risk needing 1:1, active withdrawal requiring medical inpatient, delirium, unstable vitals
  • At discharge from CSU → dated outpatient appointment, safety plan, crisis numbers, means restriction documented

Note: CSU is not a substitute for psychiatric evaluation when Tier 1A consult triggers are met.

2F · Residential treatment

Purpose: 24-hour supervised care below hospital level for patients who cannot safely live independently but do not require acute inpatient medical management.

  • 24-hour supervised care below hospital level
  • Psychiatric residential: chronic SMI with functional impairment, failed outpatient
  • SUD residential: ASAM 3.1–3.7 · Dual-diagnosis: co-occurring SUD + psychiatric
  • Duration weeks–months
Disposition logic
  • Chronic SMI + housing instability → psychiatric residential when outpatient/ACT insufficient
  • Active SUD + unsafe environment → ASAM 3.x residential; ensure psychiatric co-occurring capability
  • Acute suicide crisis → stabilize in inpatient/CSU first; residential is rarely appropriate for initial acute presentation

Note: Verify bed availability, funding source, and program scope before promising placement.

Tier 3 · Diagnosis-specific disposition
3A · Acute psychosis / schizophrenia

Purpose: Stabilize acute psychosis, rule out organic causes, and match level of care to safety and functional impairment.

  • First-episode psychosis → coordinated specialty care (e.g., NAVIGATE); early intervention improves outcomes35
  • Consider LAI before discharge for adherence risk; follow-up within 7 days; ACT, supported employment, psychosocial rehab, NAMI
Disposition logic
  • Admit → unable to self-care, dangerous behavior, command hallucinations with intent, cannot maintain safety
  • Discharge → improving, adherent or reliable supports, safe environment, follow-up arranged; low/moderate suicide risk per Tier 1A with safety plan
  • First episode → psychiatry evaluation; prefer specialty first-episode program referral; do not discharge without follow-up

Note: Exclude delirium and substance-induced psychosis before labeling primary psychotic disorder.

3B · Mania / bipolar emergency

Purpose: Treat acute mania safely, initiate mood stabilization, and prevent harm from impulsivity and poor judgment.

  • Start mood stabilizer (Li, VPA) or atypical; screen for bipolar before antidepressant (can precipitate mania)
  • MDQ: ≥7 symptoms positive (sens 73%, spec 90% in psych outpatients)37
Disposition logic
  • Admit → severe mania with dangerous behavior, psychotic features, cannot self-care, no sleep for days, high suicide risk
  • Discharge → resolving symptoms, med started, safe environment, follow-up within 7 days, safety plan if any SI
  • Borderline → PHP/IOP if contained and adheres to plan; psychiatry consult if disposition uncertain

Note: Mania impairs judgment — reassess capacity for discharge decisions.

3C · MDD with suicidality

Purpose: Treat depression while stratifying suicide risk separately — depression severity alone does not determine disposition.

PHQ-9 bands8

  • 0–4 minimal · 5–9 mild · 10–14 moderate (MDD cutoff; sens/spec 88%) · 15–19 mod-severe · 20–27 severe
  • ≥10 suggests MDD; ≥15 suggests active treatment; always assess Item 9 further regardless of total
  • Start antidepressant (SSRI first-line) when appropriate; follow-up within 7 days; consider 24–72 h callback
Disposition logic
  • Drive disposition by C-SSRS level (Tier 1A), not PHQ-9 total or Item 9 alone
  • Low suicide risk + moderate MDD → outpatient + safety plan + meds + 7-day follow-up
  • Moderate suicide risk → PHP/IOP, 48–72 h follow-up, psychiatric evaluation if assessor lacks training
  • High suicide risk or psychotic depression → inpatient; psychiatric evaluation required

Note: Item 9 positive is a screen — not a discharge clearance by itself.

3D · Substance intoxication

Purpose: Medically stabilize intoxication, reassess psychiatric status when sober, and link to SUD care when indicated.

  • Alcohol: observe until BAL declining + ambulating safely, protect airway
  • Stimulant: monitor cardiac, agitation (benzos preferred), hyperthermia
  • Opioid/overdose: naloxone, observe ≥2 h post-naloxone (longer for long-acting), offer MOUD
Disposition logic
  • Discharge when → clinically sober, stable vitals, no SI when sober (per Tier 1A), safe plan, SUD screen + referral offered
  • Do not discharge → persistent AMS, abnormal vitals, SI when sober, unsafe environment, severe withdrawal emerging
  • Psychiatric disposition while intoxicated → defer final risk rating until sober when feasible; maintain observation

Note: Intoxication lowers inhibition — reassess suicide and violence risk after sobriety.

3E · Substance withdrawal (CIWA-Ar, COWS, benzo)

Purpose: Prevent morbidity and mortality from withdrawal while planning appropriate ASAM level of care.

Alcohol — CIWA-Ar (10 items, 0–67)39

  • <10 mild → supportive, consider gabapentin/carbamazepine, thiamine 100 mg
  • 10–18 moderate → benzodiazepines (chlordiazepoxide/diazepam, or lorazepam if liver disease), symptom-triggered
  • ≥19 severe → inpatient; inpatient if CIWA-Ar ≥20, DTs/seizure history, complicating conditions
  • Benzos cut seizure risk 84% vs placebo for CIWA-Ar ≥10

Opioid — COWS (11 items, 0–47)41

  • 5–12 mild · 13–24 moderate · 25–36 mod-severe · >36 severe
  • Buprenorphine induction at COWS ≥10 (target 12–16); 2–8 mg SL, repeat 2–4 mg q4h; IDU/mod-severe OUD often tolerate initial 8 mg
  • Precipitated-withdrawal risk with full agonists (fentanyl/methadone); not life-threatening but drives relapse — offer MOUD before discharge

Benzodiazepine27

  • Can be life-threatening (seizures); high-dose → taper in inpatient/medically managed residential (ASAM 3.7/4); gradual over weeks–months, no abrupt stop
Disposition logic
  • CIWA-Ar ≥20 or seizure history → medical inpatient / ASAM 4 — not psychiatric facility alone
  • Mild opioid withdrawal → outpatient MOUD linkage; buprenorphine induction in ED when appropriate
  • Benzo dependence → do not discharge without medically supervised taper plan

Note: Withdrawal management is a medical decision first — psychiatry consult for co-occurring SI/psychosis.

3F · MOUD (medication for opioid use disorder)

Purpose: Initiate or bridge evidence-based opioid-use treatment from the ED or inpatient setting to reduce overdose mortality.

  • Buprenorphine: ED/inpatient/outpatient; X-waiver eliminated (2023); maintenance 8–24 mg/day SL; MOUD follow-up within 72 h40
  • Methadone: OUD only via licensed OTP (not outpatient Rx); many OTPs take walk-ins
  • Naltrexone: oral 50 mg/day or IM XR 380 mg/month; opioid-free 7–10 days first; also for AUD; no abuse potential
Disposition logic
  • After overdose reversal → offer buprenorphine + naloxone kit + OTP/IOP referral; observe per protocol
  • Before discharge on buprenorphine → named follow-up within 72 h; warn about precipitated withdrawal if used with full agonists
  • Active SI → address Tier 1A suicide risk first; MOUD does not replace psychiatric disposition

Note: Follow local ED buprenorphine initiation protocols and state prescribing rules.

3G · Personality disorder in crisis (BPD)

Purpose: De-escalate acute distress, ensure safety, and link to longitudinal DBT-oriented care — avoid iatrogenic reinforcement of crisis cycles.

  • Safety planning (Stanley-Brown) is primary; avoid reinforcing ED visits as coping
  • Refer to DBT (strongest evidence); consider PHP/IOP with DBT; coordinate with outpatient provider before discharge
Disposition logic
  • Brief admission/observation → only for acute safety that cannot be managed outpatient — hours to ~72 h, not prolonged stay
  • Discharge → safety plan, crisis resources, DBT/IOP referral, outpatient contact within 7 days
  • Moderate/high suicide risk → apply Tier 1A; psychiatric evaluation; PHP with DBT skills group when stable enough

Note: Validate distress while setting limits — diagnosis alone is not an admission indication.

3H · Delirium

Purpose: Recognize delirium as an acute medical neurocognitive syndrome requiring medical workup — not primary psychiatric disposition.

  • Delirium is a MEDICAL emergency — do NOT disposition to a psychiatric facility
  • Identify & treat the underlying cause (infection, metabolic, medication, withdrawal, hypoxia); manage on a medical service
Disposition logic
  • All cases → medical admission or observation until cause treated and cognition improving
  • Psychiatry role → consult for agitation, capacity, comorbid primary psych disorder — not primary disposition to psych bed
  • Before any psych transfer → confirm delirium resolved or excluded; reassess capacity and suicide risk when cleared

Note: CAM-positive delirium overrides psychiatric disposition planning until medically addressed.
